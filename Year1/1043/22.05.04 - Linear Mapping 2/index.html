Rank-Nullity Theorem

Let f:E→Ff:E\to Ff:E→F be a linear mapping. The rank of fff is the dimension of the image, rk(f):=dim(Im(f))rk(f) := dim(Im(f))rk(f):=dim(Im(f)) The rank is an invariant of the map.

The dimension of the kernel, dim(ker(f))dim(ker(f))dim(ker(f)) is said nullity of a mapping.

Theorem​

Under the hypothese: the sum of rank and nullity of a mapping is equal to the dimension of the vector space (E,+,.)(E,+,.)(E,+,.): dim(ker(f))+dim(Im(f))=dim(E)dim(ker(f)) + dim(Im(f)) = dim(E)dim(ker(f))+dim(Im(f))=dim(E) Usually dim(Im(f))dim(Im(f))dim(Im(f)) is the hardest to calculate and this theorem allows an easy way to compute it as dim(E)−dim(ker(f))dim(E)-dim(ker(f))dim(E)−dim(ker(f))

Proof​

If dim(ker(f))=0dim(ker(f))=0dim(ker(f))=0 then fff is injective. If the basis spans the image, the vectors are linearly independent.

Introduction to Diagonalization

One variable for each line in a diagonal manner. Not all the mappings/matrices can be diagonalized, Need to have enough linearly independent columns of PPP.

Let f:E→Ef:E\to Ef:E→E be an endomorphism. The matrix is diagonalizable if and only if it has n linearly independent eigenvectors:

  • All the eigenvalues are distinct
  • The algebraic multiplicity of each eigenvalue coincides with its geometric multiplicity

Symmetric Mappings​
